Unit 01 · Topic A

Biomolecules

The four classes of macromolecules: structure, monomer–polymer relationships, and biological roles.

Macromolecule 01

Carbohydrates

Biomolecules used for energy and structural support in living organisms.

Elements & Structure

Carbohydrates are made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in a ratio of roughly 1C : 2H : 1O — for example, glucose has the formula C₆H₁₂O₆.

Monomer → Polymer

The monomer is a monosaccharide (one sugar). Two monomers form a disaccharide. Many monomers chain together via dehydration synthesis to form a polysaccharide. All monosaccharides and disaccharides end in "-ose."

How They Bond

Carbohydrates bond to each other through dehydration synthesis — water is removed as two smaller molecules join to form a larger one. The reverse (adding water to break the bond) is called hydrolysis.

Examples
  • Glucose — the most common energy source for nearly all organisms; a monosaccharide
  • Fructose — fruit sugar; a monosaccharide found naturally in fruits and honey
  • Lactose — milk sugar; a disaccharide (glucose + galactose)
  • Sucrose — table sugar; a disaccharide (glucose + fructose)
  • Ribose / Deoxyribose — monosaccharides that form the backbone of RNA and DNA
  • Starch — polysaccharide; how plants store excess carbohydrates (e.g. potatoes, rice, bread)
  • Cellulose — polysaccharide used by plants for structural support in cell walls (e.g. wood, cotton fiber)
  • Chitin — polysaccharide used by insects and fungi for in insections and fungi.
Key Functions Breaking down carbohydrates provides organisms with energy. Excess carbs are stored as starches (long chains of polysaccharides). Plants also use cellulose for structural support — a different polysaccharide made from the same glucose monomer.
Macromolecule 02

Lipids

Hydrophobic molecules used for energy storage, waterproofing, chemical signaling, and insulation.

Elements & Structure

Lipids are made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. The ratio is roughly 1C : 2H — oxygen is present only in trace amounts. Unlike the other biomolecules, lipids have no true monomer.

Functional Groups

Most lipids are built from two parts: glycerol (an alcohol with three oxygen groups) and fatty acids (long hydrocarbon chains). All lipids repel water — they are hydrophobic and do not bond to water molecules.

Fats (Triglycerides)

All fats are triglycerides — three fatty acids bonded to one glycerol molecule. Examples: butter, lard, olive oil, vegetable oil, body fat (adipose tissue).

Four Key Functions + Examples
  • Storing large amounts of energy — body fat (adipose tissue) stores far more energy per gram than carbohydrates; e.g. fat reserves in bears before hibernation
  • Waterproof barriers — the waxy cuticle on plant leaves; the oily coating on a duck's feathers; earwax
  • Chemical messengers (hormones) — steroids are lipids with four rings bonded together; examples include testosterone, estrogen, cortisol, and cholesterol
  • Insulation — the blubber layer in whales and seals; myelin sheath insulating nerve fibers
Steroids Steroids are a special class of lipid defined by four carbon rings bonded together. They function as hormones — chemical signals used throughout the body. Examples: testosterone (muscle development), estrogen (reproductive signaling), cortisol (stress response), cholesterol (cell membrane structure).
Macromolecule 03

Proteins

Vital for structural support, enzymes, transport, fighting infection, and much more.

Elements & Structure

All proteins contain carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen. Sulfur may also be present. The monomer is an amino acid; the polymer is a protein (also called a polypeptide — a chain of amino acid chains).

How Amino Acids Bond

Amino acids are joined through dehydration synthesis. The C-group of one amino acid binds to the N-group of the next, forming a peptide bond.

Four Phases of Protein Production
  • Primary — amino acids are bound together into a chain
  • Secondary — individual amino acids are bent and molded as needed
  • Tertiary — the entire chain is bent and folded into a subunit
  • Quaternary — multiple completed subunits are fitted together to make the complete protein
Functions & Examples
  • Structural support — keratin (hair, nails, skin); collagen (tendons, ligaments, skin); actin (muscle fibers)
  • Enzymes — speed up chemical reactions; e.g. amylase (breaks down starch in saliva), lactase (breaks down lactose), DNA polymerase (copies DNA)
  • Transport — hemoglobin carries oxygen in red blood cells; channel proteins move molecules across cell membranes
  • Fighting infection — antibodies identify and neutralize pathogens like bacteria and viruses
  • Hormones — protein hormones (e.g. insulin, glucagon) must bind a cellular receptor before they can affect the cell, since they have difficulty crossing the membrane
Protein Hormones vs. Steroid Hormones Protein hormones cannot easily pass through the cell membrane — they must fit a cellular receptor on the cell surface to deliver their signal. Steroid hormones (lipids) can pass directly through the membrane.
Macromolecule 04

Nucleic Acids

Blueprints for making proteins and transmitting genetic information to the next generation.

Elements — Remember "CHONP"

Nucleic acids contain Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, and Phosphorus. Use the acronym CHONP to remember all five elements.

Monomer: The Nucleotide

Every nucleotide has three parts: a 5-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base. Many nucleotides chain together to form a nucleic acid polymer.

DNA vs. RNA
  • DNA — stores the blueprints for making proteins; carries hereditary information passed from parent to child; uses deoxyribose sugar
  • RNA — reads and carries out the DNA blueprints to build proteins; uses ribose sugar; cannot be passed down to offspring
Examples & Functions
  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) — found in the nucleus; contains the genetic instructions for every protein your cells make; passed from parents to offspring (hereditary traits)
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid) — carries instructions from DNA to the ribosome so proteins can be built; not passed on to offspring
  • mRNA (messenger RNA) — the specific RNA molecule that carries the protein-building code out of the nucleus
  • ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — a special nucleotide that serves as the cell's main energy-carrying molecule
Heredity DNA is the only biomolecule that can be passed from parent to child. This is what makes traits hereditary — eye color, blood type, and many other characteristics are encoded in DNA and inherited. RNA carries out the instructions but is not passed to offspring.
