Unit 01 · Topic A

Biomolecules

The four classes of macromolecules: structure, monomer–polymer relationships, and biological roles.

Macromolecule 01

Carbohydrates

Biomolecules used for energy and structural support in living organisms.

Elements & Structure

Carbohydrates are made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in a ratio of roughly 1C : 2H : 1O — for example, glucose has the formula C₆H₁₂O₆.

Monomer → Polymer

The monomer is a monosaccharide (one sugar). Two monomers form a disaccharide. Many monomers chain together via dehydration synthesis to form a polysaccharide. All monosaccharides and disaccharides end in "-ose."

How They Bond

Carbohydrates bond to each other through dehydration synthesis — water is removed as two smaller molecules join to form a larger one. The reverse (adding water to break the bond) is called hydrolysis.

Examples
  • Glucose — the most common energy source for nearly all organisms; a monosaccharide
  • Fructose — fruit sugar; a monosaccharide found naturally in fruits and honey
  • Lactose — milk sugar; a disaccharide (glucose + galactose)
  • Sucrose — table sugar; a disaccharide (glucose + fructose)
  • Ribose / Deoxyribose — monosaccharides that form the backbone of RNA and DNA
  • Starch — polysaccharide; how plants store excess carbohydrates (e.g. potatoes, rice, bread)
  • Cellulose — polysaccharide used by plants for structural support in cell walls (e.g. wood, cotton fiber)
  • Chitin — polysaccharide used by insects and fungi for in insections and fungi.
Key Functions Breaking down carbohydrates provides organisms with energy. Excess carbs are stored as starches (long chains of polysaccharides). Plants also use cellulose for structural support — a different polysaccharide made from the same glucose monomer.
Macromolecule 02

Lipids

Hydrophobic molecules used for energy storage, waterproofing, chemical signaling, and insulation.

Elements & Structure

Lipids are made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. The ratio is roughly 1C : 2H — oxygen is present only in trace amounts. Unlike the other biomolecules, lipids have no true monomer.

Functional Groups

Most lipids are built from two parts: glycerol (an alcohol with three oxygen groups) and fatty acids (long hydrocarbon chains). All lipids repel water — they are hydrophobic and do not bond to water molecules.

Fats (Triglycerides)

All fats are triglycerides — three fatty acids bonded to one glycerol molecule. Examples: butter, lard, olive oil, vegetable oil, body fat (adipose tissue).

Four Key Functions + Examples
  • Storing large amounts of energy — body fat (adipose tissue) stores far more energy per gram than carbohydrates; e.g. fat reserves in bears before hibernation
  • Waterproof barriers — the waxy cuticle on plant leaves; the oily coating on a duck's feathers; earwax
  • Chemical messengers (hormones) — steroids are lipids with four rings bonded together; examples include testosterone, estrogen, cortisol, and cholesterol
  • Insulation — the blubber layer in whales and seals; myelin sheath insulating nerve fibers
Steroids Steroids are a special class of lipid defined by four carbon rings bonded together. They function as hormones — chemical signals used throughout the body. Examples: testosterone (muscle development), estrogen (reproductive signaling), cortisol (stress response), cholesterol (cell membrane structure).
Macromolecule 03

Proteins

Vital for structural support, enzymes, transport, fighting infection, and much more.

Elements & Structure

All proteins contain carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen. Sulfur may also be present. The monomer is an amino acid; the polymer is a protein (also called a polypeptide — a chain of amino acid chains).

How Amino Acids Bond

Amino acids are joined through dehydration synthesis. The C-group of one amino acid binds to the N-group of the next, forming a peptide bond.

Four Phases of Protein Production
  • Primary — amino acids are bound together into a chain
  • Secondary — individual amino acids are bent and molded as needed
  • Tertiary — the entire chain is bent and folded into a subunit
  • Quaternary — multiple completed subunits are fitted together to make the complete protein
Functions & Examples
  • Structural support — keratin (hair, nails, skin); collagen (tendons, ligaments, skin); actin (muscle fibers)
  • Enzymes — speed up chemical reactions; e.g. amylase (breaks down starch in saliva), lactase (breaks down lactose), DNA polymerase (copies DNA)
  • Transport — hemoglobin carries oxygen in red blood cells; channel proteins move molecules across cell membranes
  • Fighting infection — antibodies identify and neutralize pathogens like bacteria and viruses
  • Hormones — protein hormones (e.g. insulin, glucagon) must bind a cellular receptor before they can affect the cell, since they have difficulty crossing the membrane
Protein Hormones vs. Steroid Hormones Protein hormones cannot easily pass through the cell membrane — they must fit a cellular receptor on the cell surface to deliver their signal. Steroid hormones (lipids) can pass directly through the membrane.
Macromolecule 04

Nucleic Acids

Blueprints for making proteins and transmitting genetic information to the next generation.

Elements — Remember "CHONP"

Nucleic acids contain Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, and Phosphorus. Use the acronym CHONP to remember all five elements.

Monomer: The Nucleotide

Every nucleotide has three parts: a 5-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base. Many nucleotides chain together to form a nucleic acid polymer.

DNA vs. RNA
  • DNA — stores the blueprints for making proteins; carries hereditary information passed from parent to child; uses deoxyribose sugar
  • RNA — reads and carries out the DNA blueprints to build proteins; uses ribose sugar; cannot be passed down to offspring
Examples & Functions
  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) — found in the nucleus; contains the genetic instructions for every protein your cells make; passed from parents to offspring (hereditary traits)
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid) — carries instructions from DNA to the ribosome so proteins can be built; not passed on to offspring
  • mRNA (messenger RNA) — the specific RNA molecule that carries the protein-building code out of the nucleus
  • ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — a special nucleotide that serves as the cell's main energy-carrying molecule
Heredity DNA is the only biomolecule that can be passed from parent to child. This is what makes traits hereditary — eye color, blood type, and many other characteristics are encoded in DNA and inherited. RNA carries out the instructions but is not passed to offspring.

Quick Memory Tips
  • Carbs end in "-ose" — glucose, fructose, sucrose, lactose, maltose
  • Enzymes end in "-ase" — these are proteins that catalyze reactions
  • Lipids have no true monomer — they're built from glycerol + fatty acids (or 4-ring steroids)
  • "CHONP" — the only macromolecule containing Phosphorus is a nucleic acid

  • DNA vs. RNA — DNA uses deoxyribose and is heritable; RNA uses ribose and carries out instructions
Bonding: Dehydration Synthesis & Hydrolysis

All macromolecules (except lipids) are built by dehydration synthesis — water is removed as monomers join. The reverse reaction, hydrolysis, adds water to break the polymer apart. Lipids bond the same way but are not considered true polymers.

Protein Levels of Organization Proteins pass through four structural levels: Primary (amino acid chain) → Secondary (local folding: α-helices, β-sheets) → Tertiary (full 3D shape of one subunit) → Quaternary (multiple subunits assembled). Enzymes are at the tertiary or quaternary level.
Why Lipids Are Different Unlike the other three macromolecules, lipids are defined by being hydrophobic (water-repelling) rather than by a shared monomer. They are grouped together because they all repel water, not because they share a repeating subunit.

Unit 01 · Topic B

Enzymes

Protein catalysts, induced-fit model, enzyme kinetics, and the effect of temperature, pH, and inhibitors.

Enzymes 01

What Are Enzymes?

Biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions without being permanently changed.

Definition & Structure

Most enzymes are proteins at the tertiary or quaternary structural level. They act as biological catalysts — they speed up chemical reactions by lowering the energy needed to start them. Crucially, enzymes are not permanently changed in the process and can be used over and over.

Naming Convention

Almost all enzyme names end in "-ase", usually derived from the substrate they act on:

  • Sucrase — breaks down sucrose (table sugar)
  • Lactase — breaks down lactose (milk sugar)
  • Maltase — breaks down maltose (malt sugar)
  • Amylase — breaks down starch (in saliva)
  • DNA Polymerase — builds new DNA strands
Key Properties
  • Specificity — each enzyme acts on only one specific substrate, like a lock and key
  • Reusable — after the reaction finishes, the enzyme is released unchanged and can bind a new substrate
  • Catalyst — speeds up a reaction without being consumed or permanently altered
  • Protein-based — most are proteins (tertiary/quaternary structure), though some RNA molecules (ribozymes) also act as enzymes
Enzymes in Your Body Right Now Your saliva contains amylase that begins breaking down starch as you chew. Your stomach uses pepsin to digest proteins. Your small intestine uses lipase, protease, and more amylase to complete digestion. Thousands of enzyme-catalyzed reactions run simultaneously in every cell.



Enzymes 02

Activation Energy

Enzymes lower the energy barrier that must be overcome to start a reaction.

How Enzymes Lower Activation Energy

Every chemical reaction requires an initial input of energy — called activation energy — to get started, even reactions that ultimately release energy. Enzymes work by weakening bonds in the substrate, which lowers this energy barrier and allows reactions to proceed much faster at body temperature.

Without vs. With an Enzyme
  • Without enzyme — reactants must climb a steep energy hill before products can form; very slow at body temperature
  • With enzyme — the energy hill is dramatically lowered; the same reaction happens thousands of times faster
  • The overall energy change of the reaction (products vs. reactants) is unchanged — the enzyme only changes the speed, not the outcome
Key Insight The enzyme does not change whether a reaction is energetically favorable — it only changes how fast it happens. The products and overall energy released are identical with or without the enzyme.


Enzymes 03

Enzyme–Substrate Complex

The substrate binds to the active site, forming a temporary enzyme–substrate complex.

Substrate & Active Site

The molecule an enzyme acts on is called the substrate. The enzyme has a specialized region called the active site — a restricted pocket or groove with a specific 3D shape. When the substrate enters the active site, the enzyme–substrate complex forms temporarily, and the reaction occurs. The enzyme then releases the product and is free to bind another substrate.

Steps of a Catalyzed Reaction
  • Step 1 — Free enzyme (active site open) encounters substrate in solution
  • Step 2 — Substrate binds the active site → enzyme–substrate complex forms
  • Step 3 — Chemical bonds in the substrate are broken or formed
  • Step 4 — Products are released; enzyme returns to its original shape
  • Step 5 — Enzyme is free to catalyze the next reaction
Specificity — Lock & Key Because the active site has a very specific shape, most enzymes can only bind one type of substrate. This is called enzyme specificity. For example, sucrase will only bind sucrose — it cannot bind lactose or starch.


Enzymes 04

Induced Fit Model

The active site flexes and adjusts its shape when the substrate binds — like a glove conforming to a hand.

What Is Induced Fit?

The original Lock-and-Key model imagined the active site as a rigid, pre-formed pocket. The more accurate Induced Fit model shows that the active site is flexible. When the substrate approaches, the active site slightly changes its 3D configuration — involving hydrogen bonds and ionic bonds — to grip the substrate more tightly. This conformational change helps catalyze the reaction.

Lock-and-Key vs. Induced Fit
  • Lock-and-Key (old model) — active site is rigid; substrate fits perfectly without any change
  • Induced Fit (current model) — active site is flexible; substrate induces a conformational change in the enzyme that tightens the fit and accelerates catalysis
Why This Matters The induced fit mechanism explains why enzymes are such effective catalysts — the conformational change actually stresses bonds in the substrate, making them easier to break or rearrange, further lowering the activation energy.


Enzymes 05

What Affects Enzyme Activity?

Temperature, pH, and ionic concentration can all slow or permanently destroy an enzyme.

1. Temperature

Enzymes have an optimal temperature at which they work fastest. As temperature rises toward the optimum, reaction rate increases. However, excessive heat disrupts the hydrogen bonds that maintain the enzyme's 3D shape, causing it to denature (unfold) and lose function permanently.

2. pH

Most enzymes work best near neutral pH (6–8). Each enzyme has a specific optimal pH. Extreme pH disrupts ionic and hydrogen bonds, denaturing the enzyme. Example: pepsin works best at pH 2 (stomach acid), while trypsin works best at pH 8 (small intestine).

3. Ionic Concentration (Salt Ions)

High concentrations of ions can interfere with the ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds that maintain the enzyme's active site shape, reducing or eliminating activity.

Denaturation Is (Usually) Permanent When an enzyme denatures, its shape is permanently destroyed — it cannot refold correctly. This is why high fever is dangerous: body temperatures above ~40°C can begin denaturing critical enzymes. This is also why cooking destroys enzymes in food.



Enzymes 06

Enzyme Inhibitors

Molecules that reduce or block enzyme activity — nature's off-switch for chemical reactions.

a. Competitive Inhibitors

A competitive inhibitor is a molecule that resembles the normal substrate in shape. It competes with the real substrate for the same active site. If the inhibitor occupies the active site, the substrate cannot bind and the reaction cannot occur.

  • Blocks the active site directly
  • Can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration — more substrate can out-compete the inhibitor
  • Example: Sulfa drugs compete with bacterial enzymes; ibuprofen competitively inhibits COX enzymes to reduce inflammation
b. Noncompetitive Inhibitors

A noncompetitive inhibitor does not bind the active site. Instead, it attaches to a different region of the enzyme (an allosteric site), causing the enzyme's shape to change so the active site is distorted — the substrate can no longer fit.

  • Does not compete with the substrate for the active site
  • Cannot be overcome by adding more substrate
  • Example: Heavy metal poisoning (lead, mercury) — metals bind allosteric sites and permanently alter enzyme shape
Cofactors & Coenzymes Some enzymes need helpers to work: cofactors (inorganic ions like iron or zinc) and coenzymes (organic molecules, often vitamins). Example: hemoglobin needs iron in its quaternary structure to carry oxygen. Without the cofactor, the enzyme cannot function.
